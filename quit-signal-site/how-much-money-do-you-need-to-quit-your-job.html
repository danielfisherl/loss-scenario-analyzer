How Much Money Do You Need to Quit Your Job?

The right answer is not a fixed number. It depends on how much monthly burn you are actually carrying, how much of that burn you can cut, what healthcare will cost after you leave, and whether any income is likely during the break.

For high-earning tech workers, the useful question is not “How much have I saved?” It is “How many months of real freedom does that savings buy before I need a bad next move?”

The Short Answer

Most people should think in terms of months of runway, not a single savings number. A 12-month runway is a good baseline. Eighteen months is safer if your costs are high, your job search may take time, or you want room to recover before you decide what is next.

What Actually Matters

  • Monthly burn: the number that determines how fast cash disappears.
  • Healthcare delta: COBRA or ACA can materially change the answer.
  • Household income: partner income or side income can extend runway.
  • Unvested bonus or equity: near-term payouts can make waiting worthwhile.
  • Job search friction: specialized roles often need more runway than generic ones.

The Formula

Required savings = (monthly post-quit spend + healthcare increase - other income - break income) x runway target + one-time costs

If you want a more conservative version, use current spend instead of post-quit spend first. That shows how much room you have before you make any lifestyle cuts.

Plug-And-Play Examples

  • Lean lifestyle: if you can lower post-quit spend and have some household income, a smaller savings number can still buy a meaningful runway.
  • Middle case: a typical tech household usually needs a real cushion because rent, insurance, food, and taxes do not disappear when the paycheck does.
  • High-burn case: if you are carrying a mortgage, childcare, or family coverage, the required number rises fast even when income is high.

What A Good Answer Looks Like

For most analytical people, a good answer sounds less like “you can quit” and more like “you can quit if you accept this runway and these tradeoffs.” That is useful because it turns the question from vague fear into a specific decision.

Common Mistakes

  • Using salary instead of monthly burn.
  • Ignoring healthcare until after you quit.
  • Counting unvested RSUs as liquid cash.
  • Thinking one more year is always safer without measuring the cost.
