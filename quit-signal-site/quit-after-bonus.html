Should I Quit After My Bonus?

Decision memo · Updated March 2026 · Built from the same runway logic as the calculator

For many tech workers, a bonus is the last meaningful payout before leaving. The right move depends on whether that payout materially improves your runway enough to justify staying.

If the bonus only changes the answer a little, it should not control the decision. If it materially extends runway or lowers anxiety about the next move, waiting can be rational.

The Short Answer

Wait for the bonus if the after-tax payout is large relative to your liquid savings and the waiting period is short. Quit sooner if the bonus is small, the role is getting worse, or your runway remains weak even after the payout.

When Waiting For The Bonus Makes Sense

  • The after-tax bonus is large relative to your liquid cash.
  • The wait is short, usually a few weeks or a couple of months.
  • Your job is tolerable enough that staying a little longer is not harmful.
  • The bonus is enough to move you from “possible but risky” to “stronger signal.”

When Quitting Before The Bonus Makes Sense

  • You are already burned out or unhealthy.
  • The bonus is small compared with your savings.
  • The role is getting worse, not better.
  • Waiting would still leave you with weak runway.

Example Scenarios

  • Short wait, large payout: If a 2-month wait adds $40k after tax and your runway is thin, waiting is usually worth serious consideration.
  • Short wait, small payout: If a 2-month wait adds $8k after tax, the bonus may not justify another round of stress.
  • High burnout, decent runway: If your finances are already stable, the bonus might be irrelevant compared with the cost of staying.

How To Think About It

The real question is not whether the bonus exists. It is whether the bonus changes the decision enough to justify the extra time. For a high-earning person, that means measuring the after-tax amount against the cost of staying, not the headline number on the comp letter.

What People Miss

  • Bonuses are taxed, so the usable number is always smaller than the headline.
  • Waiting can also change the market risk you face when you finally leave.
  • A bonus does not fix a weak runway if your monthly burn is still too high.
