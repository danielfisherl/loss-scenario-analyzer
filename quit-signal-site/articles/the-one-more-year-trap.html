The One More Year Trap

Decision memo - Updated March 2026 - Built for the time-off-work calculator

The one more year trap happens when staying feels rational in the short term, but the extra year quietly becomes the next year. It is one of the most common ways high earners delay a decision without explicitly choosing to stay.

The trap is not always irrational. Sometimes waiting really does buy meaningful cash or reduce risk. The problem is when the phrase becomes a holding pattern instead of a plan.

How to model it

Write down the exact thing you would gain by waiting: a bonus, a vest, a severance package, a lower spend target, or more time to line up a return path. Then subtract the cost of staying one more year in energy, health, and optionality. If the trade is small, the extra year may be more expensive than it looks.

Three ways this trap shows up

  • You keep waiting for a vague better market, even though no concrete milestone is attached to the wait.
  • You tell yourself the next bonus or vest will change everything, but the actual after-tax value is not large enough to move the decision.
  • You are really trying to buy certainty, which is not something an extra year can fully provide.

What changes the answer?

  • The trap weakens when you assign a date and a dollar amount to the wait.
  • A specific bonus or vest can justify staying. A vague hope usually cannot.
  • If you would not actually leave after the extra year, you are probably not making a plan yet.

Common mistake

  • Common mistake: treating “one more year” like a neutral delay instead of a real decision with a cost.
  • Another mistake is refusing to model the emotional cost of staying while insisting on precision for every financial variable.

What to do next

If you are stuck here, define the exact condition that would make you leave. Put a number next to it and a date on the calendar. If you cannot do that, the issue may be less about the money and more about not wanting to decide yet.
