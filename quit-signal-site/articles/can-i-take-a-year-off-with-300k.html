Can I Take a Year Off Work With $300k Saved?

Decision memo - Updated March 2026 - Built for the time-off-work calculator

$300k creates real flexibility for a one-year break, but the answer still depends on your spending pattern and how much reserve you want to keep. The difference between “possible” and “comfortable” is usually not the savings number itself. It is the monthly burn underneath it.

For many households, $300k means a year off is on the table. For high-cost households, it can still feel surprisingly tight once housing, healthcare, and lifestyle are all included.

A grounded scenario

If your monthly burn is around $8,000 to $10,000, $300k can fund a year with room left over, especially if you are not traveling aggressively. If your monthly burn is closer to $15,000 or higher, the margin shrinks quickly and the break starts depending on optimism about the future.

What changes the answer?

  • The break becomes much safer if you cut spending before leaving.
  • Bonus or severance inflow can push this from workable to comfortable.
  • A one-year break is sensitive to healthcare and housing costs because they repeat every month, not once.

Common mistake

  • Common mistake: assuming $300k is universally enough for a year off without modeling the actual household burn.
  • Another mistake is treating any leftover amount as free money instead of leaving a buffer for the return transition.

Reality check

$300k often gets you close to a yes, but the right answer depends on whether you want the break to feel intentional or merely survivable. If the latter, the plan is usually too brittle.
