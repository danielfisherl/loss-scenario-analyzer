The One More Year Trap

The one more year trap happens when staying feels rational in the short term, but each extra year quietly becomes the next year and the next one after that. It is one of the most common ways high earners postpone a decision without ever explicitly choosing to stay.

The trap is not irrational by default. Sometimes one more year really does buy meaningful money or reduce risk. The problem is when the phrase becomes a holding pattern instead of a decision.

How To Model It

Compare the value of staying for one more year against the cost of losing a year of freedom, health, and optionality. Then subtract the real financial upside, like a bonus or vest. If the remaining value is small, you may be paying a large emotional cost for a modest financial gain.

A Simple Version Of The Math

Value of waiting = bonus or vest + extra savings + reduced near-term risk - cost of staying - delay in life you actually want

You do not need a perfect estimate. You just need enough structure to stop using “one more year” as a vague placeholder for fear.

Questions To Ask

  • What exact dollar amount do I gain by waiting?
  • What exact cost do I pay in stress and burnout?
  • Would I actually quit after that one more year?
  • What would need to be true for me to stop saying this again next year?

Three Versions Of The Trap

  • Specific and bounded: wait for a bonus or vest, then quit. This can be rational.
  • Specific but drifting: wait one more year, then discover the goalpost moved.
  • Vague and endless: keep saying one more year because the decision itself feels expensive.

Common Failure Mode

People often say “one more year” when they really mean “I am afraid to make the decision now.” The fix is to put the numbers into a calculator, set a date, and define what would cause you to leave at that point.

What A Better Decision Looks Like

A better decision is specific. It names the cash amount, the timing constraint, and the condition under which you will actually act. If the answer is still no after the numbers are visible, that is useful. If the answer is only “maybe later,” the trap is still running.
